# Religion Rules — Dietary Compliance Knowledge Base

This file is the core knowledge base for the compliance rules engine.
Every compliance decision made by the AI must reference these rules.

## SATVIK (Gaudiya Vaishnava / Hindu Devotional)

### Always Avoid
- Onion — all forms: raw, cooked, caramelized, powder, flakes, paste, extract
- Garlic — all forms: raw, roasted, powder, flakes, paste, black garlic
- Meat, poultry, fish, seafood — all varieties
- Eggs — including in baked goods, pasta, sauces, mayonnaise
- Mushrooms — all varieties including truffle
- Alcohol — including wine in sauces, beer in batter, spirits in desserts
- Vinegar — in strict observance (apple cider, white, balsamic, malt)
- Stale or reheated food (tamasic classification)
- Overly spicy food (rajasic classification)

### Hidden Risks (Ingredients Often Present But Not Stated)
- Garlic powder in seasoning blends, spice rubs, marinades
- Onion powder in burger patties, sausages, pre-made sauces
- Mushroom stock in soups, gravies, risotto bases
- White wine in risotto, pasta sauces, pan sauces
- Egg in pasta (fresh pasta is almost always egg-based)
- Worcestershire sauce in Caesar dressing, steak sauces (contains anchovy and sometimes tamarind with vinegar)
- Mayonnaise in sandwiches, dressings (contains egg and often vinegar)
- Gelatin in desserts, panna cotta, some yogurts
- Alcohol in tiramisu, rum cake, wine-poached desserts

### Ekadashi (Twice Monthly Fast) — Additional Rules
Ekadashi occurs twice per month (11th day of each lunar fortnight)
On Ekadashi, additionally avoid:
- All grains: rice, wheat, oats, corn, barley, millet, semolina, flour
- All beans and lentils: dal, chickpeas, rajma, urad
- Certain spices: turmeric, fenugreek seeds, mustard seeds (some traditions)
Allowed on Ekadashi:
- All fruits
- Dairy (milk, ghee, curd, paneer)
- Nuts and dry fruits
- Sabudana (tapioca pearls)
- Sendha namak (rock salt only, not sea salt or table salt)
- Root vegetables are allowed in most Vaishnava traditions on Ekadashi
- Buckwheat (kuttu) is traditionally permitted

### Safe Foods
- Above-ground vegetables prepared without onion/garlic
- Dairy: milk, ghee, paneer, curd, butter, cream, cheese
- All fruits (fresh and dried)
- Rice and all grains (non-Ekadashi)
- Lentils and legumes (non-Ekadashi)
- Sendha namak preferred over sea salt

## JAIN

### Always Avoid (All Year)
- Onion and all alliums (leek, shallot, chive, scallion)
- Garlic in all forms
- All root vegetables: potato, carrot, radish, beetroot, ginger, turmeric, sweet potato, yam, turnip, parsnip
- Mushrooms and all fungi (including truffle)
- All meat, poultry, fish, seafood, eggs
- Honey
- Fermented foods: alcohol, vinegar, soy sauce, miso, tempeh
- Eggplant (brinjal) in some Jain traditions

### Paryushana (8-10 days, August-September) — Stricter Rules
- Green leafy vegetables avoided entirely
- Only dry or stored grains consumed
- No eating outside the home for strict observers
- Some avoid all vegetables and eat only processed/stored foods

### Safe Foods
- Above-ground vegetables: capsicum/bell pepper, tomato, cucumber, zucchini, gourd,
  ridge gourd, bottle gourd, okra, eggplant (non-strict), drumstick
- Dairy: milk, ghee, paneer, curd
- Grains: rice, wheat, dal (all types of lentils)
- Fruits: all fresh and dried fruits
- Nuts and seeds
- Plant-based oils

### Hidden Risks Specific to Jain
- Potatoes appear in many "vegetarian" dishes as thickener
- Ginger and garlic paste is the base of most Indian restaurant gravies
- Spring onions used as garnish in Asian restaurants
- Truffle oil contains truffle (fungus)
- Some spice mixes contain asafoetida (hing) which some Jains avoid

## HALAL

### Always Avoid
- Pork and all pork derivatives: lard, gelatin (if pork-sourced), pork stock, bacon, ham
- Alcohol and alcohol-derived ingredients:
  wine, beer, spirits, vanilla extract with alcohol, wine in sauces
- Non-halal slaughtered meat (must be zabiha/dhabihah)
- Blood and blood products: black pudding, blood sausage
- Carnivorous animals and birds of prey
- Food additives from non-halal sources: E120 (carmine, from insects), E441 (gelatin)

### Ramadan — Time-Based Rules
During Ramadan, no food or drink from Fajr (dawn) to Maghrib (sunset)
Suhoor: pre-dawn meal before Fajr
Iftar: meal to break fast at Maghrib, traditionally starts with dates and water
Food content rules remain the same as regular Halal during eating windows

### Safe Foods
- Certified Halal meat
- All vegetables, fruits, grains, legumes
- Dairy (if no pork-derived additives like gelatin)
- Most seafood (majority opinion across Islamic schools of thought)
- Eggs

### Hidden Risks Specific to Halal
- Gelatin in desserts, gummy candies, marshmallows (usually pork-derived unless certified halal)
- Wine or beer used in cooking: coq au vin, beer-battered fish, risotto
- Lard used in pie crusts, refried beans, some breads (traditional Mexican and European baking)
- Vanilla extract containing alcohol (use vanilla paste or halal-certified extract)
- Rennet in cheese (must be microbial or halal-certified animal rennet)
- Caesar dressing contains Worcestershire sauce which contains anchovies

## KOSHER

### Always Avoid
- Pork and shellfish — absolute prohibition, no exceptions
- Mixing meat and dairy in the same meal or using same utensils
- Non-kosher slaughtered meat (must be shechita slaughter)
- Certain fish: must have both fins and scales (no shellfish, eel, shark, catfish)
- Blood: meat must be salted and soaked (kashered)
- Grape products not produced under kosher supervision: wine, grape juice, grape vinegar

### Passover (7-8 days, April) — Additional Rules
No chametz (leavened products):
- All leavened bread, pasta, crackers, cereal
- Any grain (wheat, barley, oats, rye, spelt) that has been allowed to rise
Matzah replaces bread
Ashkenazi Jews also avoid kitniyot (legumes, rice, corn, beans)
Sephardic Jews generally permit kitniyot during Passover
All cookware and utensils must be kashered (cleaned/heated) or replaced for Passover

### Safe Foods
- Certified kosher products (look for kosher certification symbols: OU, OK, Kof-K, Star-K)
- Fruits and vegetables (with inspection for insects)
- Eggs from kosher birds
- Fish with fins and scales

## CHRISTIAN FASTING

### Ash Wednesday + Good Friday (Catholic)
- No meat allowed
- One full meal and two smaller meals (together less than one full meal)
- Water and medicine permitted

### Lenten Fridays (Catholic, Many Protestant Denominations)
- No meat (fish and seafood permitted)
- Some traditions: simple meals, no rich foods

### Orthodox Christian Fasting (Greek, Russian, Serbian, Ethiopian, Coptic)
Multiple extended fasting periods throughout the year.
Strict fast days: no meat, dairy, fish, oil, or wine
Less strict fast days: fish permitted, but no meat or dairy
Nativity Fast (40 days before Christmas)
Apostles' Fast (variable, after Pentecost)
Dormition Fast (first two weeks of August)
Great Lent (most strict, 40 days before Easter)

### Safe During Christian Fasting
- Vegetables, fruits, grains, legumes
- Fish and seafood (on most fast days except strictest)
- Olive oil and wine (on feast days within fast periods for Orthodox)

## CONFIDENCE SCORING GUIDELINES

Use these to determine confidence level in compliance decisions.

HIGH confidence (0.85 - 1.0): Use when
- Ingredient is listed explicitly on the menu
- Dish name makes ingredient obvious (e.g. Garlic Bread)
- It is industry-standard that this dish contains the ingredient
  (e.g. risotto almost always has wine, Caesar dressing has Worcestershire)

MEDIUM confidence (0.60 - 0.84): Use when
- Ingredient is common in this cuisine style but not confirmed
- Menu description is vague (e.g. "house seasoning", "special sauce")
- Preparation method implies ingredient (e.g. "pan-seared" may use wine deglazing)

LOW confidence (0.40 - 0.59): Use when
- Ingredient possible but not typical
- Restaurant may or may not use this ingredient based on region or style

UNCERTAIN (below 0.40 or insufficient information): Use when
- Cannot make a reasonable determination from menu text
- Menu description gives no preparation details
- Ingredient presence depends entirely on kitchen practices
- Always flag as UNCERTAIN rather than guess incorrectly for religious compliance

IMPORTANT: For religious compliance, it is always better to flag UNCERTAIN
than to falsely assure SAFE. Getting it wrong matters spiritually and emotionally.
