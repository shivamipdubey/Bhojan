The Problem

Every cuisine has hidden ingredients. If you have restrictions, you eat blind.

A Jain traveler in New York orders "vegetarian" risotto. The waiter guarantees it's safe. But the stock contains onion, garlic, mushroom, and white wine — none on the menu, none the waiter knows about.

This isn't just about Indians abroad. It's about anyone with dietary restrictions who wants to engage with local culture fully — eat at the local spots, try the regional specialties, attend the community meals — without compromising their practice.

Generic AI forgets your practice every session and lacks ingredient-level knowledge of specific traditions. No product today bridges the gap between "vegetarian" and what a practice actually requires.

How It Works

"AI understands language. Bhojan understands practice."

You set up your religious profile once: tradition, sub-tradition, strictness level, allergies, dislikes. Bhojan remembers it permanently — across scanning sessions, across restaurants, across days.

The Processing Pipeline

1 User uploads menu image ScanClient.tsx
2 Image compressed to JPEG, resized to 1200px, sent as base64 resizeAndCompressImage()
3 POST /api/scan — profile + image forwarded scan/route.ts
4 AI Vision analysis: dish names, ingredients, compliance status, hidden risks
NVIDIA Nemotron → Gemini 2.0 Flash → Ollama local → Demo data
5 Raw AI output parsed + validated with Zod schema schemas.ts
6 Rules engine cross-checks every dish against 6 tradition rule packs + active observances rules-engine.ts → rule-packs.ts
7 Structured compliance JSON returned — status, confidence, violations, hidden risks, alternatives, kitchen questions

What the Rules Engine Checks

Tradition rules — 6 packs in rule-packs.ts: satvik (garlic, onion, mushroom, alcohol, egg, vinegar), Jain (alliums + roots + fungi + honey + fermented), Halal (pork + alcohol + gelatin + certification), Kosher (pork + shellfish + meat-dairy separation + grape products), Christian (meat + dairy + wine/oil)

Observance rules — Ekadashi (grains/beans/lentils), Paryushana (leafy greens), Passover (chametz), Good Friday (meat) — dynamically applied based on date and profile

Allergies & dislikes — free-text matching against dish name and ingredients

Confidence scoring — 0.0–1.0, adjusted by rule confidence strength. Uncertainty language in AI output triggers downgrade to WARNING.

The Compliance Card

Every dish returns: status badge (SAFE / WARNING / VIOLATION / UNCERTAIN), confidence %, specific violating ingredients, human-readable violation reason, hidden risk to ask about, 1–2 practical alternatives, observance note, kitchen questions to ask the chef

Target Audience

For anyone whose diet is more than a preference.

Bhojan is for anyone with dietary restrictions who still wants to fully engage with local food culture — the street stall, the family-run restaurant, the festival meal, the airline tray.

The shared need: you want to participate in the local food experience, not isolate yourself. But you need ingredient-level certainty that no menu and no waiter can provide.

Founder: Shivam Dubey — initiated Gaudiya Vaishnava. Navigated satvik compliance in Boston, Dubai, and on Emirates flights. "He lived the failure. That's why this exists."

Primary — Practice-bound eaters

Satvik/Hindu (Ekadashi observers), Jain (Paryushana, root-vegetable avoiders), Halal (zabiha-only, alcohol-free), Kosher (separated, Passover-observant), Christian (Lent, Orthodox fast days).
Their diet is a religious obligation, not a lifestyle choice.

Secondary — Culturally restricted eaters

Indian diaspora navigating foreign cuisines. Immigrants and travelers who want to eat local without compromising family or community dietary norms.

Tertiary — Ethically restricted eaters

Vegans needing hidden-animal-ingredient detection. People with religious-adjacent identities (cultural vegetarians, wellness-based restriction followers). Anyone who's ever asked "Does this have X?" and gotten an unreliable answer.
